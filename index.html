History of Ambria

A unified chronicle of the known world, from the Mythic Era to the present day. Dates given in B.G. (Before the Gates) and A.G. (After the Gates).

I. THE MYTHIC ERA
B.G. 1000 – B.G. 500  ·  The world is a sieve between realms
B.G. ~1000
ORIGIN

The Bleeding

The boundary between the Fae Realm and the Mortal World grows thin as cloth. The Lords of Light and Shadow walk openly among men, and mortality is treated as a curiosity rather than a tragedy.

B.G. 840
PROSPERITY

The First Flowering

Humanity records the first instance of "Glow-Wheat" — a Fae-touched crop that fed entire nations on a single acre. The famine that had defined early human civilization effectively ends.

B.G. 800
MAGIC

The Spark

The first human sorcerers emerge, learning to siphon ambient energy leaking from the Fae Realm. They are regarded with wonder and mild suspicion by the Fae Courts, who view them as children playing with fire.

B.G. 750
CULTURE

The Song-Halls

In this era of abundance, humans build the first grand communal Song-Halls — temples not to gods but to the Fae themselves. Minstrels channel raw ambient magic into music, and some songs are said to last for days without the singer drawing breath.

B.G. 612
DIPLOMACY

The Pact of Iron

Early humans and the Fae Courts ratify a formal agreement: shared stewardship of Davokar. In exchange for preserving the old groves, humans receive the right to build permanent settlements along Fae-touched ley lines.

B.G. 580
DIPLOMACY

The Pact of Petals

A deeper accord between mankind and the Fae Courts ushers in an era of unnatural abundance. Seasonal droughts are ended by Fae rain-callers. Human lifespans quietly lengthen near ley-line settlements.

II. THE AGE OF TYRANNY
B.G. 500 – B.G. 160  ·  The rise and fall of the Sorcerer Kings
B.G. 480
FOUNDING

The Rise of the Cabal

Twelve master conjurers unify the warring human tribes through arcane might, establishing the first Glass Citadel at Hexos. The Pact of Petals is quietly set aside as the Cabal argues that men no longer need Fae patronage.

B.G. 450
MAGIC

The Silencing of the Song-Halls

The Cabal outlaws ambient-siphon magic practiced in the old Song-Halls, declaring it "inefficient." Official arcane academies replace them. The foundation of Ordo Magica. The first institutionalized magical hierarchy is born — and with it, the first magical underclass.

B.G. 400
CREATION

The Weave-Warps

The Sorcerer Kings begin "flesh-shaping" — the creation of chimeras, guardian beasts, and bound servitor creatures. Many of these constructs escape into the deep wilds during the Great War and breed true. Their descendants still haunt forgotten places.

B.G. 320
CREATION

The Dwarven Genesis

The first batch of 1,000 Dwarves is carved from the Living Stone of the Titans in the Great Forges of Deep-Hold. They emerge fully formed — flesh and blood — but shaped by the Kings' will into beings of extraordinary physical endurance and an almost compulsive capacity for labor. Each carries within them a "motive spark," a sliver of bound ambient mana that ties their vitality to the Cabal's power. They are people in every meaningful sense. That is precisely what makes what was done to them monstrous.

B.G. 290
CONFLICT

The Compact of Refusal

A coalition of human lords in the outer provinces formally refuses to pay tribute to the Cabal, citing the abuse and suppression of old-ways magic. The Cabal responds by dispatching Stone-Wardens — dwarven war machines — to crush the rebellion. Seeds of the Great War are sown.

B.G. 240
HUBRIS

The Hubris of Hyrsam

Sorcerer King Malakor attempts to "shackle" a Fae Arch-Lord — specifically the entity known as the Lord of Unending Autumn — to power an eternal engine. The attempt partially succeeds, which is somehow worse than failure. The Fae Courts convene in fury.

B.G. 210
WAR

The Great War Begins

The Unholy Alliance forms: human lords who refused the Cabal's yoke join forces with the Fae Courts. The war is unlike anything men have known — Fae armies do not march in lines but move like weather, appearing without warning and vanishing before dawn.

B.G. 195
WAR

The Drowning of Peleth

The Glass Citadel of Peleth, built over an underground river, is destroyed when Fae water-callers redirect the river upward through the citadel's own ley-line conduits. Three thousand Cabal loyalists drown in an hour. The Sorcerer Kings begin to fracture.

B.G. 175
WAR

The Siege of Sky-Reach & The Scouring

The final Glass Citadel falls when the Fae Courts "withdraw their breath" from its walls, causing it to literally dissolve. To ensure the Kings can never return, the Fae perform a ritual that permanently scars the ley lines, making high-level conjuration volatile and deadly.

B.G. 170
AFTERMATH

The Scattering of the Dwarves

With the Cabal destroyed, the compulsions woven into the Dwarves' very flesh begin to fade — but do not vanish entirely. Many fall into profound directionlessness: they were made to serve a purpose that no longer exists, and no one told them who they are without it. Small clans withdraw into the Titans. In the silence of their halls, with no masters watching, the first whispers of a true Dwarven culture begin — their own songs, their own rituals, their own tongue. The knowledge of how they were made is deliberately buried by the victorious Fae Courts.

III. THE GOLDEN INTERLUDE
B.G. 160 – 0 A.G.  ·  Peace, reconstruction, and the peak of safe magic
B.G. 160
PEACE

The Reconstruction Begins

Peace is brokered between the Fae Courts and the surviving human lords. The modern capitals of Ambria are built atop the ruins of the old world — their foundations ancient citadel stones, their windows lit by Fae-glass that still glows faintly today.

B.G. 110
CULTURE

The Great Library of Daros

Founded at the Fae Courts' recommendation to preserve "Clean Magic" — the gentler, cooperative sorceries that survived the Scouring. The Library grows to house over 80,000 scrolls and becomes the intellectual heart of the known world.

B.G. 90
SOCIETY

The Fae-Walkers

A new class of diplomats emerges: humans trained to walk between realms and act as liaisons to the Fae Courts. Their children are the first to be born with natural Fae-marks — faint silver irises, a slight luminescence at dusk. They are celebrated. No one imagines this will one day make them targets.

B.G. 45
PROSPERITY

The Age of Artifice

The peak of magical architecture. Floating gardens irrigate the Midlands. Self-lighting lamps are common in every major city. The Dwarves, their deepest grief slowly giving way to something more like cautious curiosity, begin cautiously re-engaging with the wider world — trading labor and stonecraft for the peace of being left alone to tend what culture they are quietly building.

B.G. 20
OMEN

The Autumn Without End

For three consecutive years, autumn never fully gives way to winter in the Crownlands. Fae scholars record it as a symptom of "Gate Fatigue" — the stress of maintaining the boundary between realms. Human scholars dismiss it as weather. Both are right.

0 A.G. / 0 B.G.
Midsummer Solstice
YEAR ZERO

The Snap — The Closing of the Gates

Without warning, on Midsummer Solstice, every Fae Gate in the known world slams shut simultaneously. The Silver Mist vanishes from the air overnight. The Fae are gone. The calendar resets. This is the moment from which all modern history is measured.

IV. THE AGE OF ASH & IRON
1 A.G. – 155 A.G.  ·  Fading wonders, rising industry, present day
1–20 A.G.
COLLAPSE

The Waning

Enchanted structures begin to crack and crumble without the ambient Fae energy that sustained them. The floating gardens fall over several months. Among the Dwarves, the fading ancient motive spark woven into their flesh fully dims. They grieve. They grow still. They stare at their hands and find them purposeless. Among the Feyborn Elves, a different dread takes hold. Their kind have always entered dormancy — a deep, cocoon-like sleep from which they wake transformed, further along in a life cycle that outlasts human memory. But now, in the silence left by the Snap, some who enter dormancy do not wake. No one can say whether they are becoming something new or simply fading, slowly, into nothing. The eldest Feyborn begin refusing sleep altogether. Terrified that to close their eyes is to never open them again.

12 A.G.
POLITICS

The Iron Decree & The First Dead-Dwarf

King Edric I mandates that all Dwarves remain confined to their citadels, ostensibly for their "protection." The first Dead-Dwarf is found — a laborer who simply stopped one morning, sitting upright, hands still folded in his lap. Healers find no wound, no illness. His motive spark, that ancient sliver of bound magic in his flesh, had gone out like a candle. The Dwarven isolation deepens into something closer to collective mourning.

25 A.G.
SOCIETY

The First Feyborn

The first generation of "Stranded" Fae-descendants are born without the ability to phase back to the Fae Realm. They carry Fae-marks and trace abilities but are wholly mortal. Neither human nor Fae, they become an unwanted bridge between a world that no longer exists and one that fears what they represent.

28 A.G.
SOCIETY

The First Feyborn Census

Estimates suggest 15% of the urban population now carries some Fae descent. The first Fae-blood noble house, House Elara, is formally recognized by the Crown — a political act that inflames as many tensions as it soothes.

30 A.G.
CONFLICT

The First Feyborn Riots

In the capital, mobs blame the stranded Fae-descendants for the "thirst" of the land. Three Feyborn neighborhoods are burned. The Crown issues a tepid condemnation. It is the first blood, but not the last.

40 A.G.
RELIGION

The Founding of the Ashen Creed

In the gutted poorhouses of Daros, a lay preacher named Malakai begins teaching that magic was never a gift — it was a parasite. That the Fae bled the world dry and left. That the Feyborn carry the wound in their blood. His congregation is small, angry, and growing.

48 A.G.
RELIGION

The Sermon of the Void

The Prophet Malakai delivers his defining address to a crowd of thousands in the ruins of Daros's old Fae-Walk quarter. He argues that the Gates closed not because the Fae left but because the world expelled them. The Creed explodes in membership overnight.

55 A.G.
VIOLENCE

The Night of Broken Wands / The Great Pyre

The height of the Ashen Rebellion. Every known magical library in the Crownlands is put to the torch in a single coordinated night. Over 40% of all written magical knowledge in Ambria — including 12,000 scrolls in the Great Library of Daros — is destroyed. Scholars will spend the next century mourning what was lost.

60–62 A.G.
POLITICS

The Ashen Purge

King Edric II executes Malakai and the Creed leadership, crushing the official rebellion. The "Edict of Neutrality" is signed, forcing Feyborn to pay a Magic Tax for their inherent abilities — a cynical peace that satisfies no one. The Creed goes underground, its ideology burning on in the hearts of the common folk.

65 A.G.
RELIGION

The First Light — The Rise of Prios

In the ash-grey years following the Purge, a new faith takes root among the common people — not built on the ruins of magic, but in direct opposition to the darkness its absence left behind. Prios, the Sun God, is proclaimed: the light in the darkness, the warmth of harvest, the promise of a new dawn after the long night. His earliest preachers are not scholars or nobles but farmers, midwives, and survivors of the Great Hunger who found comfort in the idea that something still shines. The faith spreads with remarkable speed across Ambria's lowlands and market towns. By the end of the century it will become the largest organized religion the known world has ever seen — its golden solar disc hanging above every village gate, its morning prayers marking the hour of sunrise in every city from the coast to the frontier.

75 A.G.
ECONOMY

The Salt Roads Open

With elemental preservation magic gone, salt becomes the most valuable trade commodity in Ambria overnight. The merchant families who control the Southern salt flats accumulate more wealth than any noble house within a generation. The Alchemist's Guild begins its slow ascent.

85 A.G.
ECONOMY

The Rise of the Guilds

With magic unreliable and distrusted, humans turn to clockwork and alchemy. The Alchemist's Guild becomes the wealthiest single entity in Ambria, surpassing even the Crown's treasury in liquid assets. The first tensions between Guild power and royal authority begin.

100 A.G.
ECOLOGY

The Scourge of Rot

A mysterious blight strikes the northern forests — the same groves once stewarded under the Pact of Iron. Without Fae intervention, it spreads unchecked. 30% of the timber trade collapses within a decade. The resulting "Great Hunger" claims more lives than any single conflict since the Great War.

105 A.G.
INDUSTRY

The Coal Revolution

The first steam-engine is built in the southern frontier of Thistle Hold to pump water that spells used to move. It is loud, ugly, and miraculous. Within five years, six more are operating. The Dwarves - patient, and possessed of a stonecutter's instinct for how force moves through a structure — recognize something in the logic of pistons and pressure that feels almost like the old purpose. They begin trading labor and structural knowledge for the first time in generations.

110 A.G.
OMEN

The Star That Fell on Davokar

Witnesses across the southern reaches report a streak of cold fire crossing the night sky — brilliant enough to cast shadows at midnight, slow enough that watchers had time to kneel before it vanished beyond the treeline. All accounts agree it came down somewhere deep within Davokar, well past the forest's edge where roads stop and even the Freefolk are said to move carefully. No expedition sent to find the impact site has returned with anything conclusive. Scholars of Thistle Hold note with quiet unease that the Iron Knight — a constellation long associated in old Fae star-lore — appears to be missing one of its lesser stars. Whether the two facts are connected remains a matter of heated academic dispute. The Freefolk have said nothing on the matter, which most take as confirmation that they know exactly what fell, and have chosen not to share it.

120 A.G.
SOCIETY

The Reckoning of House Elara

The first Feyborn noble house is dissolved when its patriarch is found dead — officially of natural causes. No successor is named. The Magic Tax is doubled for Feyborn of noble blood in the same year. The Feyborn community reads the message clearly. Many begin passing as human.

135 A.G.
OMEN

The First Hum

Scholars at Thistle Hold report hearing a "harmonic vibration" emanating from Davokar. The first recorded Glow-Sighting in a century — stones in the old Fae-Walk districts briefly illuminating at midnight. The Academy dismisses it. The Feyborn do not.

140–142 A.G.
OMEN

The Fae Gates & The First Tremors

Old ruins begin to vibrate with no seismic cause. Sleepers report dreaming of a woman's voice singing in a language that predates human memory — later identified as the call of the Maiden's Court. A wandering mercenary witnesses a "Fae Gate" appear in the Maiden's Court. He is not believed.

150 A.G.
CROWN

The Coronation of Edric III

Edric III takes the throne — the first king in three generations born with a Fae-mark (a faint silver iris). The Creed considers it a provocation. The Feyborn consider it a miracle. The Court considers it a problem. Edric considers it none of their business and proceeds to govern.

151 A.G.
WAR

The War of Westmark Pass

Barely a year into his reign, King Edric III faces his first trial by fire. The Caer Myr Empire — Ambria's ancient neighbor to the west — begins pushing troops through the narrow passes of Westmark, claiming old territorial rights that most Ambrian scholars consider fiction. Edric calls upon the knights of the realm to ride. They answer. The war that follows is brutal, fought in freezing mountain passes where cavalry is nearly useless and supply lines collapse in the snow. Many great knights die with their banners still flying. When the last blade is sheathed, not a single inch of ground has changed hands. The war ends precisely where it began. Westmark Pass remains Ambrian — technically. The cost in blood, coin, and knightly prestige haunts the Court still, and Caer Myr has not apologized.

152 A.G.
POLITICS

The Freefolk Compact — Declaration of Davokar

Deep within Davokar — the vast, ancient forest to the south where many believe the Fae's magic never truly left — the people known as the Freefolk have long lived beyond Ambria's reach. Now their Outlaw King formally declares independence from the Crown, and the declaration carries teeth. The Freefolk are not petitioners. They are not border lords filing grievances. They are a sovereign people who have always governed themselves, and they are done pretending otherwise. In the cities of Ambria, the news lands like a cold blade. People fear the Freefolk — fear what lives in Davokar alongside them, fear the old magic they are said to carry unbothered, fear a king with no name on any Crown record who rules a forest that has swallowed armies whole.

153 A.G.
ORDER

The Founding of the Twilight Friars

As magical corruption and rogue sorcery grow bolder in the shadow of Edric's illness, the monastic order known as the Twilight Friars is formally chartered — more commonly called the Black Cloaks. Seated in Daros, they operate as both hunters of magical corruption and the Crown's foremost intelligence apparatus, domestic and foreign alike. Their mandate is given fresh urgency by a phenomenon that has begun spreading fear through every village and city in Ambria: the dead are rising. Not in great armies, not with purpose — but singly, quietly, clawing up from graves and crypts with empty eyes and no memory of how they were resurrected. The Church of Prios condemns the phenomenon with thunderous certainty, declaring it an abomination against the Sun God's natural order, a corruption born of old Fae poison bleeding back into the world. Priests of Prios march alongside Black Cloak investigators in the field, their golden solar discs held aloft like weapons. Their spokesperson, Solon Raze, is often heard declaring: "Whilst the Templars aim their swords towards Ambria's outer enemies, the knife points of the Black Cloaks are aimed at those within."

154 A.G.
CATASTROPHE

The Erasure of Withersby

Traveling merchants following the southeastern trade roads consult their maps and find Withersby marked plainly — a village of several hundred souls, a waystation known for its grain stores and decent ale. What they find instead is dust. Not rubble. Not ash. Not the wreckage of fire or war. Dust, and silence, and the faint outlines of foundations where buildings once stood. No bodies. No livestock. No crows. Nothing that suggests anything fought or fled. The village of Withersby and every living thing within it has simply ceased to exist. Word reaches the capital within a fortnight. Speculation tears through the kingdom like fever: a rogue sorcerer of unimaginable power, a Fae weapon left dormant since the Snap, a curse called down from Davokar, divine punishment, the first sign of something vast and patient waking in the ruins.

155 A.G.
Present Day
NOW

The World Holds Its Breath

King Edric III is bedridden. The Freefolk hold the forests of Davokar under their Outlaw King's banner. The Maiden's Court is sighted in three different provinces on the same night. A Gate in the Southern Frontier is "bleeding" light for the first time in 155 years. The ruins are glowing. The Interlude is truly over and a new age begins.
